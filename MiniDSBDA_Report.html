Abstract

Bias in recruitment and hiring processes is a persistent and critical challenge in modern organizations. When automated machine learning systems are used to assist in candidate screening and selection, they can inadvertently perpetuate or even amplify pre-existing societal biases present in historical data. This project presents a comprehensive framework for detecting and quantifying bias in recruitment systems using Machine Learning.

A synthetic recruitment dataset of 1,000 candidates was generated, incorporating features such as gender, age, education level, and years of experience. A deliberate gender bias was introduced in the hiring probability function to simulate real-world discriminatory patterns. A Random Forest Classifier was then trained on this dataset to predict hiring decisions.

Bias was measured using three standard fairness metrics: Disparate Impact (DI), Statistical Parity Difference (SPD), and Hiring Rate by Protected Group. The 80% rule (four-fifths rule) was applied to determine whether the model's selection rates violated legal fairness thresholds. Feature importance analysis was performed to identify which attributes most strongly influenced the model's predictions.

Results confirmed that the hiring model exhibited statistically significant gender bias, with male candidates being selected at a higher rate. The Disparate Impact score fell below the 0.8 threshold, clearly indicating bias. This project demonstrates that even seemingly neutral ML models can encode discriminatory outcomes, and that rigorous fairness auditing is essential before deploying such systems in real-world recruitment pipelines.

Keywords: Bias Detection, Recruitment, Machine Learning, Fairness Metrics, Disparate Impact, Statistical Parity Difference, Random Forest, Hiring Discrimination

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 Background

The recruitment process is one of the most consequential activities in any organization—it determines who gets access to employment opportunities and, by extension, economic resources, professional development, and social mobility. Traditionally, hiring decisions have been made by human recruiters who, despite best intentions, are often influenced by unconscious cognitive biases. These biases may favor or disfavor candidates based on attributes such as gender, age, ethnicity, or educational background, rather than on objective merit-based criteria.

With the rapid digitization of HR processes, many organizations have turned to Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) systems to automate candidate screening, résumé shortlisting, and interview scheduling. While these systems promise greater objectivity and efficiency, they are trained on historical data that often reflects past discriminatory practices. Consequently, these automated systems can perpetuate and even amplify the very biases they were designed to eliminate.

A well-publicized example is Amazon's internal AI recruiting tool, which was found in 2018 to systematically downgrade résumés from women because the model had been trained on historical hiring data predominantly reflecting male hires in technical roles. This incident highlighted the urgent need for bias detection and fairness auditing in ML-assisted recruitment systems.

1.2 Problem Definition

The central problem addressed in this project is the detection of demographic bias in ML-based hiring decision systems. Specifically, we focus on gender bias—the tendency for a trained classifier to assign higher hiring probabilities to candidates of one gender over another, independent of relevant qualifications like experience and education.

Given a dataset of recruitment records with features including gender, age, education level, and years of experience, the goal is to:

  1. Train an ML classifier to predict hiring outcomes.
  2. Evaluate the model's performance using standard classification metrics.
  3. Quantify any bias in hiring predictions using established fairness metrics.
  4. Identify which features contribute most to biased outcomes.
1.3 Motivation

The motivation for this project stems from both ethical imperatives and legal requirements. Discriminatory hiring practices violate anti-discrimination laws in many jurisdictions, including India's Constitution (Article 15 and 16), the Equal Pay Act, and the United States' Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964).

Beyond legal compliance, organizations with diverse and inclusion-focused hiring practices have been shown to outperform their peers in terms of innovation, employee engagement, and profitability. By detecting bias early—before models are deployed in production—organizations can rectify algorithmic inequities and build more fair and effective recruitment pipelines.

1.4 Objectives

The primary objectives of this project are:

  • To generate a realistic synthetic recruitment dataset incorporating intentional gender bias.
  • To train a Random Forest Classifier to predict hiring decisions based on candidate features.
  • To detect and quantify bias using Disparate Impact (DI) and Statistical Parity Difference (SPD).
  • To visualize hiring rate disparities between protected and unprotected demographic groups.
  • To analyze feature importance to understand the primary drivers of model predictions.
  • To provide a reusable, extensible framework for bias auditing in any recruitment dataset.
1.5 Scope of the Project

This project focuses on binary classification (hired vs. not hired) and binary gender representation (male vs. female) for simplicity and reproducibility. The scope includes:

  • Synthetic data generation with controllable bias parameters.
  • Machine learning model training and evaluation.
  • Application of the 80% Rule (four-fifths rule) as the primary fairness threshold.
  • Feature importance analysis using the Random Forest's built-in impurity-based mechanism.
  • Visualization of results as exportable charts and Excel reports.

The scope does not cover real-world personally identifiable data, debiasing or mitigation techniques, or intersectional analysis across multiple protected attributes simultaneously—these are identified as future directions.

Literature Survey
2.1 Bias in AI-Driven Recruitment Systems

Bias in machine learning systems has been extensively studied in recent years. Barocas and Moritz (2016) provided one of the foundational analyses of how proxy discrimination arises when sensitive attributes (such as gender or race) are correlated with seemingly neutral features (such as zip code or school attended) in training data. They argued that even when a sensitive attribute is explicitly excluded from the input features, a model can still learn to discriminate through correlated proxies.

Dastin (2018) reported on Amazon's internal experiment with an AI recruitment tool that penalized résumés containing the word "women's" (e.g., "women's chess club captain"). This high-profile case illustrated how historical gender imbalance in a workforce can embed itself in a model's learned weights, leading to systematic disadvantage for female applicants.

Raghavan et al. (2020) conducted a comprehensive audit of ten commercial hiring algorithms and found that most vendors did not disclose their fairness criteria or provide mechanisms for bias testing. This lack of transparency in commercial systems motivated the development of open, auditable frameworks such as the one presented in this project.

2.2 Fairness Metrics in Machine Learning

The ML fairness literature has produced a rich taxonomy of fairness definitions. The three most widely cited are:

2.2.1 Disparate Impact (DI)

Disparate Impact, rooted in legal doctrine from Griggs v. Duke Power Co. (1971), measures the ratio of the selection rate of a protected group to that of the unprotected group. The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's "four-fifths rule" stipulates that a DI ratio below 0.8 constitutes evidence of adverse impact:

DI = (Selection Rate of Protected Group) / (Selection Rate of Unprotected Group)

A DI value of exactly 1.0 indicates perfect parity. Values below 0.8 or above 1.25 indicate potential bias.

2.2.2 Statistical Parity Difference (SPD)

Statistical Parity Difference measures the absolute difference in selection rates between groups:

SPD = P(Hired | Female) − P(Hired | Male)

An SPD of 0 indicates perfect statistical parity. Negative values indicate lower selection rates for the protected group.

2.2.3 Equal Opportunity and Equalized Odds

Hardt et al. (2016) proposed Equalized Odds, requiring that both the True Positive Rate (TPR) and False Positive Rate (FPR) be equal across demographic groups. Equal Opportunity is a relaxation requiring only equal TPR. These metrics go beyond group-level selection rates and examine the model's internal error structure.

2.3 Related Work

Datta et al. (2015) demonstrated that Google's online advertising system showed ads for high-paying executive positions significantly more frequently to male users than to female users, providing early empirical evidence of discriminatory algorithmic output in employment-related contexts.

Feldman et al. (2015) introduced the concept of certifiably fair or accurate classifiers, proposing a repair algorithm that modifies feature distributions to satisfy disparate impact constraints while preserving as much predictive accuracy as possible.

IBM's AI Fairness 360 toolkit (Bellamy et al., 2018) provides a comprehensive suite of fairness metrics and bias mitigation algorithms, including Reweighing, Prejudice Remover, and Adversarial Debiasing. While this project implements fairness metrics from scratch for educational clarity, the AIF360 framework represents the state of the art for production-level fairness auditing.

Chouldechova (2017) revealed what is now known as the impossibility theorem of fairness: when base rates differ between groups, it is impossible to simultaneously satisfy calibration, false positive parity, and false negative parity. This finding has profound implications for how fairness metrics should be selected and interpreted in hiring contexts.

Methodology
4.1 Data Preprocessing
4.1.1 Handling Categorical Features

The education_level feature is a nominal categorical variable with four levels: High School, Bachelors, Masters, and PhD. Tree-based algorithms like Random Forest can handle label-encoded categoricals, but to avoid imposing an artificial ordinal relationship, One-Hot Encoding (OHE) was applied using pandas.get_dummies() with the drop_first=True parameter to avoid multicollinearity (dummy variable trap). This transforms the single categorical column into three binary indicator columns: education_level_High School, education_level_Masters, and education_level_PhD.

4.1.2 Feature Scaling

Random Forest classifiers are inherently scale-invariant because they partition feature space using threshold comparisons rather than distance metrics. Consequently, standard scaling (e.g., Min-Max or Z-score normalization) was not applied to the numerical features (age and experience_years).

4.1.3 Train-Test Split

The dataset was divided into a 70% training set (700 samples) and a 30% test set (300 samples) using stratified splitting to maintain the class distribution of the target variable in both subsets. A random state of 42 was used for reproducibility.

4.2 Model Implementation
4.2.1 Random Forest Classifier

A Random Forest Classifier was selected as the primary model due to its robustness to overfitting, its native ability to compute feature importances, and its strong baseline performance on tabular classification tasks. The model was instantiated with default hyperparameters (100 estimators, Gini impurity criterion) using Scikit-learn's sklearn.ensemble.RandomForestClassifier.

Random Forests operate by constructing an ensemble of Decision Trees, each trained on a bootstrap sample of the training data and a random subset of features at each split node. The final prediction is made by majority voting across all trees, which reduces variance and improves generalization.

4.2.2 Model Training and Prediction

The model was fit on the training partition using model.fit(X_train, y_train). Predictions on the held-out test set were generated using model.predict(X_test). Both class predictions (0/1) and predicted probabilities were obtained for threshold-agnostic evaluation via the ROC-AUC metric.

4.3 Bias Detection Framework
4.3.1 Group-wise Hiring Rate Analysis

The first step in bias detection is computing the selection rate (hiring rate) for each demographic group. Using the groupby function in Pandas, the mean of the hired column was computed separately for male (gender=0) and female (gender=1) subgroups in the test set.

4.3.2 Disparate Impact Calculation

The Disparate Impact ratio was computed as:

DI = Selection_Rate_Female / Selection_Rate_Male

Under the 80% rule, if DI < 0.8, the model is considered to exhibit adverse impact against female candidates. If DI > 1.25, male candidates face adverse impact. A DI in [0.8, 1.25] is considered acceptable.

4.3.3 Statistical Parity Difference

The Statistical Parity Difference was computed as the arithmetic difference in selection rates:

SPD = Selection_Rate_Female − Selection_Rate_Male

An SPD of 0 represents perfect fairness. In a biased system, SPD will be negative if the protected group (females) are hired at a lower rate, reflecting direct disadvantage.

4.3.4 Feature Importance Analysis

Random Forest provides a built-in measure of feature importance based on the mean decrease in Gini impurity across all trees and all split nodes. Features that appear frequently and high in the trees, producing large reductions in impurity, receive higher importance scores. These scores were extracted via model.feature_importances_ and visualized as a horizontal bar chart to identify which features most influence hiring predictions.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1 Conclusion

This project successfully demonstrated the design and implementation of a bias detection framework for ML-assisted recruitment systems. A synthetic dataset of 1,000 candidate records was generated with a controlled gender bias, and a Random Forest Classifier was trained to predict hiring decisions. Fairness auditing using Disparate Impact (DI = 0.724) and Statistical Parity Difference (SPD = −0.114) conclusively identified significant gender bias in both the data and the model's learned behaviour.

Key findings include:

  • ML models trained on biased historical data faithfully replicate and propagate that bias.
  • Gender ranked as the second most important feature in the Random Forest model, despite being a legally protected attribute.
  • The 80% fairness rule (Disparate Impact < 0.8) provides a clear, legally grounded threshold for bias assessment.
  • Standard accuracy metrics (64% overall) can mask serious fairness violations—a model can appear performant while systematically discriminating against a demographic group.

This project underscores the principle that fairness and accuracy are orthogonal objectives in supervised learning, and that optimizing solely for predictive performance can lead to ethically unacceptable outcomes. Organizations deploying ML in hiring must treat fairness auditing as a mandatory step in the ML lifecycle.

6.2 Future Work

Several promising directions exist for extending this work:

6.2.1 Bias Mitigation

The natural next step is to implement bias mitigation techniques. Pre-processing approaches (e.g., Reweighing, Disparate Impact Remover), in-processing approaches (e.g., Prejudice Remover Regularizer, Adversarial Debiasing), and post-processing approaches (e.g., Equalized Odds post-processing) can be evaluated and compared for their effectiveness in reducing DI while preserving accuracy.

6.2.2 Integration with AIF360 / Fairlearn

IBM's AI Fairness 360 and Microsoft's Fairlearn provide production-ready implementations of over 70 fairness metrics and 10 debiasing algorithms. Future work should integrate these toolkits to enable comprehensive, standardized fairness reporting.

6.2.3 Intersectional Fairness

This project examined gender bias in isolation. Real-world discrimination often operates at the intersection of multiple protected attributes (e.g., gender × ethnicity, age × disability). Future work should extend the framework to measure intersectional fairness, which is considerably more complex but essential for comprehensive auditing.

6.2.4 Real-World Dataset Validation

While synthetic data provides perfect experimental control, validation on real-world recruitment datasets (e.g., the COMPAS dataset adapted for hiring, or publicly available HR analytics datasets from Kaggle) would strengthen the practical relevance of the findings.

6.2.5 Explainability with SHAP Values

SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) provide individual-level feature attribution, enabling auditors to explain why a specific candidate was recommended or rejected by the model. Integrating SHAP with the bias framework would provide candidate-level fairness explanations, a key requirement for regulatory compliance under the EU's proposed Artificial Intelligence Act.
